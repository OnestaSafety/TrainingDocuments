Ladder Safety
Training Reference

A field-level reference for site superintendents, carpenters, and laborers. Covers WA L&I and federal OSHA requirements for ladder selection, setup, inspection, and safe use on general contracting and restoration job sites.

01 — Context

Why Ladder Safety Matters

Falls from ladders are one of the leading causes of fatalities and serious injuries in construction. In Washington State and nationally, ladder incidents account for a disproportionate share of workers' compensation claims — and nearly every one of them is preventable.

300+
Workers die annually from ladder falls in the U.S. (BLS/CPSC)
164K+
Emergency room visits per year due to ladder-related injuries
81%
Of ladder injuries occur in construction and maintenance occupations
Key insight: Most ladder injuries don't happen on long, complex jobs at extreme heights. They happen during short-duration tasks — hanging drywall, accessing a roof hatch, reaching a ceiling — where the crew member felt the risk wasn't worth a proper setup. That assumption is exactly what causes the injury.

The three most common causes of ladder injuries on construction sites:

  • 1
    Improper setup — wrong angle, unstable base, ladder too short, or leaned against an unsecured surface.
  • 2
    Unsafe behavior while climbing — overreaching, carrying tools in-hand, skipping rungs, or losing three-point contact.
  • 3
    Using a damaged or wrong-type ladder — defective rungs or rails, incorrect duty rating, or aluminum near electrical sources.
02 — Critical Rules

Six Non-Negotiables

These six rules apply to every ladder use, every time — regardless of task duration, height, or experience level. There are no exceptions on an Onestà job site.

Right Ladder for the Job
Select the correct ladder type and height for the task. Never use a step ladder as an extension ladder or vice versa. The ladder must reach the work point without standing on the top rungs.
Three Points of Contact
Two hands and one foot, or two feet and one hand, must be in contact with the ladder at all times while climbing or descending. Never release all three contact points simultaneously.
Proper Angle (1:4 Rule)
Extension ladders must be set at a 75° angle — 1 foot out from the base for every 4 feet of working height. Too steep tips backward; too shallow causes the feet to slide out.
Secure the Ladder
Whenever possible, tie off the top and/or bottom to a fixed anchor. At minimum, have a second person foot the ladder when it cannot be secured. Never leave an unsecured ladder in a traffic area.
Never Stand on Top Step
The top cap and top two rungs of a step ladder, and the top three rungs of an extension ladder, are prohibited standing positions. These place your center of gravity above the ladder's support envelope.
Fiberglass Near Electricity
Aluminum conducts electricity. Any time there is a possibility of contact with energized equipment, power lines, or live circuits — fiberglass is required. Assume all lines are live.
03 — Regulatory

WA L&I & Federal OSHA Requirements

Ladder safety is regulated by both Washington State Labor & Industries and federal OSHA. Violations can result in citations, fines, stop-work orders, and increased L&I audit exposure.

WAC 296-155-480
Ladders — General Requirements
Washington's primary ladder regulation for construction work sites. Covers ladder condition, load ratings, setup requirements, fall protection thresholds (6 ft in construction), and inspection obligations.

29 CFR 1926.1053
OSHA — Ladders (Construction)
Federal OSHA's ladder standard for construction. Specifies angle requirements, extension above landing (3 ft), rung spacing (10–14 in), load capacity, and prohibition on use during lightning or high winds.

29 CFR 1926.1060
OSHA — Ladder Training
Requires employers to train each employee on fall hazards, correct procedures for erecting/using ladders, maximum load capacities, and inspection. Training must be conducted by a competent person.

Enforcement note: Falls from elevation are one of OSHA's "Fatal Four." WA L&I has authority to issue fines up to $70,000 per willful violation. The fall protection threshold in Washington State construction is 6 feet — more protective than general industry (4 ft).

Who Is Responsible?

Ladder safety is a shared obligation. OSHA and WA L&I impose specific duties on both workers and supervisors — and those duties cannot be delegated away.

  • ✓Keep belt buckle between the side rails at all times

05 — Equipment

Types of Ladders

Each ladder type is designed for specific applications. Using the wrong type for a task isn't a minor shortcut — it's a setup failure that creates real fall risk. Know what you're picking up before you carry it to the job.

Step Ladder
Self-supporting, A-frame design. Used when no wall or structure is available for leaning. Available in 4–12 ft. Must be fully opened and locked before use. Never lean a step ladder against a wall in a closed position.
Extension Ladder
Non-self-supporting. Must be leaned against a structure at the correct angle. Used for roof access and elevated work points. Must extend 3 ft above the landing point. Requires both hands free on approach and departure.
Platform Ladder
Self-supporting with a guarded standing platform at the top. Preferred for tasks requiring prolonged standing — drywall finishing, light fixture work, painting. More stable than standard step ladders for extended tasks.
Articulating / Multi-Position Ladder
Can be configured as step, extension, scaffold base, or staircase ladder. Common on renovation and restoration sites. Only use configurations listed in the manufacturer's instructions. Hinges must fully lock before climbing.
Trestle / Scaffolding Ladders
Used as supports for scaffold planks — not standalone climbing ladders. OSHA and WA L&I have additional requirements for scaffold base ladders. Consult your competent person.
Never improvise a ladder. Stacking materials, using shelving, or standing on equipment to reach height is a violation of WAC 296-155-480. If the right ladder isn't on site, stop and get one.
06 — Load Capacity

Duty Ratings — Know Before You Climb

Every ladder has a maximum load rating that includes everything on the ladder — the worker, clothing, tools, materials, and any equipment being carried. Exceeding the rated load is a WAC and OSHA violation and can cause catastrophic structural failure.

TypeMax LoadIntended UseField Guidance
Type IAA 375 lbs Special duty industrial Highest rating available. Appropriate for workers carrying heavy tools or materials.
Type IA 300 lbs Extra heavy-duty industrial Recommended minimum for most Onestà field applications. Accounts for a 200 lb worker plus tools and materials.
Type I 250 lbs Heavy-duty industrial Acceptable for lighter workers with minimal tool load. A worker with a full tool belt can approach this limit quickly — when in doubt, use IA.
Type II 225 lbs Medium-duty commercial Not recommended for construction job sites. Too low a margin for workers with tool loads and PPE.
Type III 200 lbs Light-duty household Not permitted on Onestà job sites. Remove and replace immediately if found on site.
The math matters: A 185 lb carpenter wearing boots, a tool belt, knee pads, and carrying lumber is likely already at 240–260 lbs before stepping on a ladder. Always account for your full working load — not just your body weight.
07 — Electrical Hazards

Electrical Safety and Ladder Material

Electrocution from ladder contact with energized sources is a leading cause of ladder fatalities. Ladder material selection is one of the most critical decisions on restoration, renovation, and TI jobs where live circuits may be present or encountered unexpectedly.

Fiberglass — Required Near Electricity
Fiberglass is non-conductive. Required whenever there is any possibility of contact with energized equipment, overhead lines, exposed wiring, or live panels. This includes restoration scopes and TI work where circuits may be unexpectedly live.

When in doubt, use fiberglass.
Aluminum — Prohibited Near Electricity
Aluminum conducts electricity. Contact with an energized source can result in fatal electrocution. Never use aluminum ladders near electrical environments — panels, junction boxes, overhead utilities, or partially demolished structures.
Assume all lines are live. On restoration and renovation sites, the energized status of wiring is often unknown. Never assume a circuit is dead without confirmation from a qualified electrician and verified lockout/tagout.

Minimum approach distances — overhead power lines (OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1408):

  • !
    Up to 300V: Avoid contact. Treat as energized.
  • !
    300V to 50kV: 10-foot minimum approach distance for unqualified workers.
  • !
    Over 50kV: Distance increases beyond 10 ft — consult your competent person and the utility company before any work in proximity.
08 — Pre-Use

Ladder Inspection Before Every Use

WAC 296-155-480 requires that ladders be inspected before use — each day, before the ladder is put into service. A visual inspection takes less than 60 seconds and is your first line of defense against a fall caused by equipment failure.

Side Rails
Check for cracks, bends, dents, or corrosion on both rails. Any structural deformation = remove from service immediately.
Rungs & Steps
All rungs must be intact, evenly spaced, and free from cracks, bending, grease, oil, or dirt. Missing or broken rungs are immediate disqualifiers.
Feet / Slip-Resistant Pads
Rubber feet must be present, intact, and securely attached. Worn, hardened, or missing feet significantly increase slip risk at the base.
Locking Mechanisms
Rung locks and extension hooks must engage fully and positively. Test each locking point. A partially engaged lock can fail under load without warning.
Spreader Bars (Step Ladders)
Must be fully extended and locked before climbing. A step ladder not fully opened is structurally compromised and will collapse under load.
Duty Rating Label
Confirm the label is legible and appropriate for the planned task and user's loaded weight. Unreadable label = remove from service until identified.
Surface Cleanliness
Rungs, steps, and rails must be clean and dry. Mud, ice, drywall compound, and paint dramatically increase slip risk. Clean before use.
No Active Tags or Red-Lines
Check for existing out-of-service tags or markings. A tagged ladder is off-limits regardless of any other consideration.
Tag out immediately. If any defect is found, mark the ladder "Do Not Use" and remove it from the work area. Report to your supervisor same day.
09 — Setup

Proper Ladder Setup

A correctly chosen ladder on an improper setup is just as dangerous as a defective one. Most ladder falls begin at ground level — with a foundation that wasn't stable, level, or secured before anyone climbed.

✓Correct Setup
  • Firm, level ground — free from debris and loose material
  • Ladder feet fully in contact with the surface
  • Extension ladders extend at least 3 ft above the landing
  • Ladder tied off at top or footed by a second person
  • Set at the correct 1:4 angle (75°)
  • Positioned to avoid doorways, walkways, and traffic zones
  • Barricade or warning signs around base in high-traffic areas
  • Both spreader bars locked (step ladders)
✗Incorrect Setup
  • Soft ground, mud, ice, loose gravel, or uneven surfaces
  • Propped on boxes, buckets, lumber, or stacked materials
  • Extension ladder not reaching or barely reaching the landing
  • Unfooted, unsecured ladder left standing in a traffic area
  • Too steep or too shallow an angle
  • Placed in front of an unlocked, unmarked doorway
  • Step ladder partially open or leaned against wall while closed
  • Single side rail resting on a fragile or rounded surface
3 feet above the landing point is a hard requirement under OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1053. This overhang gives the climber something to hold as they step on and off the ladder at the landing — the most dangerous transition.
1
Evaluate the base surface

Is it firm? Level? Free from debris? Address the surface before placing the ladder — don't compensate with ladder position.

2
Position and angle the ladder

Set the base 1 foot out for every 4 feet of working height. Use the arm-length test: stand at the base, extend your arms — palms should reach the rung at shoulder height.

3
Secure top and bottom

Tie off the top to a stable anchor if possible. If not, have a second person foot the ladder — both hands and one foot on the bottom rung, facing the ladder.

4
Control the hazard zone

If the ladder is near a door, walkway, or active work area, establish a barrier, cone the area, or post a spotter.

5
Test before you climb

Give the ladder a firm test push at mid-height. It should not shift, rock, or tip. If it does, re-evaluate the base or add securing before proceeding.

10 — The 1:4 Rule

Correct Ladder Angle

The angle at which a non-self-supporting ladder is placed is one of the most critical factors in stability. OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1053 specifies the horizontal distance from the base to the vertical structure must be one-quarter the working length of the ladder.

1:4 Ratio · 75°
1 ft out for every 4 ft up

For every 4 feet of vertical working height, the base must be placed 1 foot out from the wall. A 20-foot ladder accessing a 16-foot wall should have its base 4 feet out. This produces the optimal 75° angle specified by OSHA.

Correct (75°) — Stable under load. Weight transfers properly to the base.
Too steep (>75°) — Ladder tips backward. Top slides out under load.
Too shallow (<75°) — Feet kick out. Buckling risk. Top slides down the wall.
Field test — the arm-reach method: Stand upright at the base with your toes touching the feet. Extend your arms straight forward. If your palms rest comfortably on the rung at shoulder height, the angle is approximately correct.
11 — Technique

Safe Climbing Technique

How you climb matters as much as how you set up. The majority of falls from properly set-up ladders happen because the climber broke one of these rules during ascent, work, or descent.

  • →
    Face the ladder — always. Climb and descend facing the rungs. Never climb facing outward. Chest toward rungs at all times.
  • →
    Three points of contact. Two hands + one foot, or two feet + one hand. Non-negotiable from the first rung to the last.
  • →
    Keep your belt buckle between the rails. Your center of gravity must remain within the side rails at all times. Leaning outside the rails is one of the leading causes of overturning falls.
  • →
    One person at a time. No ladder shall be occupied by more than one person at a time. No exceptions for "just a second" hand-offs.
  • →
    Move deliberately — no rushing. Grip firmly, move one rung at a time, do not skip rungs in either direction.
  • →
    Hands on rungs, not rails. Gripping the rails instead of rungs dramatically reduces your ability to catch yourself if a foot slips.
Descending is when most falls occur. Workers are more cautious going up than coming down. Take the same deliberate approach on descent — face the ladder, grip the rungs, three points of contact the entire way down.
12 — Tool Handling

Handling Tools and Materials on Ladders

Carrying tools or materials while climbing is one of the most common ways workers break the three-points-of-contact rule. OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1053 requires workers to maintain a firm grip with both hands while ascending and descending.

✓Safe Practices
  • Use a tool belt or tool pouch — hands completely free
  • Use a material hoist or rope-and-bucket for larger items
  • Pass materials to a second worker once at height with secure footing
  • Use a tool lanyard from your belt to tools needed at the work point
  • Make multiple trips rather than overload
✗Prohibited Practices
  • Carrying tools in your hands while climbing or descending
  • Shouldering lumber, pipe, or sheet goods while on a ladder
  • Tucking tools under your arm and using only one hand on rungs
  • Carrying items that restrict your field of vision while climbing
  • Tossing tools or materials up to someone on a ladder
If the item requires two hands to carry, it requires two people. One stages at the base, one at the landing. Hand off once fully positioned — never mid-climb.
13 — Prohibited Actions

What Is Never Permitted

These actions are prohibited on all Onestà job sites. They are not judgment calls or situational exceptions — they are hard stops. If you observe any of these, address them immediately.

Prohibited
Standing on the Top Step, Cap, or Top Rungs The top two steps of a step ladder and the top three rungs of an extension ladder are never to be used as standing positions. This prohibition is clearly labeled on every compliant ladder. Working from the top step places your center of gravity above the ladder's stability envelope. OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1053 violation · High fall injury risk
Prohibited
Moving or Repositioning a Ladder While Occupied A ladder must never be moved, shifted, extended, or repositioned while anyone is on it. Descend fully, reposition, re-secure, and re-inspect before re-climbing. No exceptions — including "just scooting it over a little." Immediate fall risk · Prohibited under WAC 296-155-480
Prohibited
Overreaching or Working Outside the Rail Width If your belt buckle is outside the rails, you are overreaching. Descend, reposition the ladder, and climb again. The extra two minutes is not negotiable against a serious fall injury. Primary cause of overturn and tip-over falls
Prohibited
Use in High Wind or Adverse Conditions Ladders shall not be used during high winds, active weather events, or when the work surface is icy or dangerously wet. OSHA specifically prohibits use during electrical storms. When conditions deteriorate, stop work and descend. OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1053(b)(7) · Stop work condition
Prohibited
Using a Defective or Tagged-Out Ladder A ladder identified as defective and tagged out is not to be used under any circumstances — regardless of how minor the damage appears, how urgent the task is, or what any supervisor says. No schedule pressure overrides this. WAC 296-155-480 violation · L&I citation risk
Prohibited
Using a Ladder as a Work Platform or Horizontal Brace Ladders are not to be used on their side, laid across two supports as a plank, or used as a scaffold component unless specifically designed and rated for that use. This bypasses the ladder's structural design and load path entirely. Structural failure risk · Prohibited under manufacturer guidelines
14 — Hazard Recognition

Common Hazards on Our Job Sites

General contracting, TI, and restoration work creates specific ladder hazards different from new construction. Many of these require active recognition — not just rule-following.

Wet and Contaminated Surfaces
Restoration scopes frequently involve water-damaged structures, active leaks, or wet flooring. Rungs with drywall mud, paint, or spray foam lose their slip resistance. Clean rungs before climbing. Never set ladder feet in standing water.
Uneven or Unstable Flooring
Demo scopes, structural repairs, and subfloor work can leave floor surfaces uneven, weak, or partially removed. Ladder feet placed on compromised flooring can punch through or collapse. Probe questionable surfaces before placing weight on them.
Overhead Electrical and Mechanical
TI work frequently involves overhead utilities in close proximity to ladder tops. Know what is above you before you climb. Mark energized equipment. Use fiberglass when in doubt about electrical proximity.
High-Traffic Work Areas
Active construction sites have foot traffic from multiple trades. Ladders in corridors, near doorways, and in open bays are vulnerable to being bumped by other workers or equipment movement. Barrier and communicate.
Limited-Access and Confined Spaces
Crawlspaces, attics, mechanical rooms, and shaft access points often require short ladder use in tight quarters where proper angle cannot be achieved. Evaluate whether a step stool or platform scaffold may be more appropriate.
End-of-Day Fatigue
Falls are disproportionately likely in the last two hours of a shift, when fatigue reduces grip strength, balance, and attention. Supervisors should be more watchful during this window. If you're fatigued, that's not the time to climb to 14 feet.
15 — Stop Work Authority

When to Stop Work

Every Onestà field employee has the unconditional authority to stop work when a ladder-related safety condition cannot be adequately controlled. Stop Work Authority cannot be overridden by schedule pressure, client requests, or supervisor instructions.

Stop Work Authority is unconditional. No supervisor, client, or schedule pressure overrides your right to stop an unsafe operation. Stop, report, and do not resume until the hazard is addressed.
  • !
    The ladder is defective, damaged, or tagged out — Any structural defect, missing feet, broken rungs, or existing tag is an immediate stop condition.
  • !
    The surface cannot be made stable — If the base cannot be placed on firm, level ground and no corrective measure can adequately stabilize it, the task must be re-evaluated for a different access method.
  • !
    Electrical hazards cannot be confirmed controlled — If the energized status of overhead lines, panels, or wiring cannot be confirmed through LOTO, and fiberglass is not available, stop and get the right equipment.
  • !
    Weather conditions create unacceptable risk — Active rain on an exterior metal ladder, high wind, or icing are stop conditions. These are not "use your best judgment" situations.
  • !
    A coworker is in a fall-risk position — If you observe another crew member overreaching or climbing a defective ladder, stop them immediately. A direct, respectful intervention is appropriate and expected.
After stopping: Notify your supervisor immediately. Describe what you observed. Document if it rises to the level of a near-miss or incident under the APP. Do not resume until the condition has been corrected and confirmed safe.
16 — Leadership

Supervisor Expectations

Supervisors are the most important variable in ladder safety compliance on any job site. Under OSHA 29 CFR 1926.1060, a competent person is required to oversee ladder setup and use. On Onestà sites, site superintendents are the designated competent persons for ladder safety.

  • →
    Conduct or verify pre-use inspections daily. Before ladder work begins each day, confirm that crew members have inspected their ladders. The pre-use inspection requirement applies every single day.
  • →
    Ensure correct ladder types and ratings are on site before the task begins. If the work requires a 24-foot Type IA extension ladder, that ladder must be staged before the crew starts. Improvising with what's available is a supervision failure.
  • →
    Remove defective ladders immediately — same day. A tagged ladder that stays on site because a replacement wasn't sourced is a compliance violation. Plan for replacements in your project staging.
  • →
    Correct unsafe setups and behaviors in the moment. Addressing an overreaching situation after the shift ends is not enforcement — it's documentation after the near-miss already happened. Address it when you see it.
  • →
    Document near-misses and incidents. Washington State requires near-misses and incidents be logged as part of the Accident Prevention Program. Ladder near-misses must be reported — they are free warnings about your site conditions.
  • →
    Enforce fiberglass requirements unconditionally near electrical hazards. "They're careful around it" is not an acceptable response to an aluminum ladder near a live panel. Fiberglass is a site requirement with no exceptions.
  • →
    Model the behavior. If a superintendent is observed skipping rungs, carrying tools in-hand up a ladder, or not footing a ladder, it directly undermines every safety message delivered at toolbox talks. Prepare Humbly, Perform Confidently — that applies to safety leadership first.
Start Strong, Finish Strong: Safety leadership isn't a morning task list — it's an all-day standard. The last hour of the shift is when fatigue sets in and shortcuts happen. That's when supervisor presence matters most.

Key Takeaways

If there are six things every Onestà field employee remembers from this resource, these are them.

01
Right ladder, right rating, every time. Type IA minimum on construction sites. Fiberglass near electricity. No exceptions based on job duration or task urgency.
02
Inspect before every use — every day. Rails, rungs, feet, locks, spreaders. Less than 60 seconds. Required by WAC 296-155-480. Non-negotiable.
03
Set it up correctly or don't climb. Firm ground, 1:4 angle, 3 feet above the landing, secured or footed. Shortcuts at setup are how falls begin.
04

05
